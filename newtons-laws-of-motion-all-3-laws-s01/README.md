# Newton's Laws of Motion

Team: S01

## Project Overview

This project explains Newton's three laws of motion in a simple and easy way for children.

Newton's laws help us understand how things move, why things stop, and what happens when we push or pull an object. In this project, children can learn the three laws using simple explanations, examples, animations, quizzes, and small activities.

## What Are Newton's Laws?

Newton's laws of motion are three important rules in physics. These rules explain the motion of objects around us.

We can see Newton's laws in daily life when we walk, run, ride a bicycle, kick a ball, travel in a bus, or see a rocket launch.

## Main Objectives

The main objectives of this project are:

1. To explain Newton's laws in simple language.
2. To help children understand motion, force, mass, and acceleration.
3. To show daily life examples for each law.
4. To make learning physics interesting.
5. To test learning through a quiz.

## Important Words

### Force

Force means a push or pull.

Example: Pushing a door, pulling a bag, or kicking a ball.

### Motion

Motion means movement.

Example: A moving car, a rolling ball, or a flying bird.

### Mass

Mass means how heavy an object is.

Example: A school bag has more mass than a pencil.

### Acceleration

Acceleration means change in speed.

Example: A bicycle goes faster when we pedal harder.

### Inertia

Inertia means an object does not want to change its state.

If it is resting, it wants to stay resting. If it is moving, it wants to keep moving.

## Newton's First Law of Motion

### Simple Meaning

An object will stay still or keep moving unless a force acts on it.

This law is also called the law of inertia.

### Easy Explanation

If a ball is lying on the ground, it will not move by itself. Someone must kick it or push it.

If a ball is moving, it keeps moving for some time. It stops because of forces like friction.

### Daily Life Examples

1. A book on a table stays there until someone moves it.
2. A football does not move until it is kicked.
3. When a bus stops suddenly, passengers move forward.
4. A rolling ball stops slowly because of friction.

### What Children Learn

Children learn that objects cannot start or stop moving by themselves. A force is needed to change motion.

## Newton's Second Law of Motion

### Simple Meaning

The motion of an object depends on force and mass.

The formula is:

```text
Force = Mass x Acceleration
F = m x a
```

### Easy Explanation

If we push an object harder, it moves faster.

If an object is heavy, it is harder to move.

### Daily Life Examples

1. An empty shopping cart is easy to push.
2. A full shopping cart is harder to push.
3. A ball moves faster when we kick it harder.
4. A heavy box needs more force to move than a light box.

### What Children Learn

Children learn that more force gives more movement, and heavy objects need more force to move.

## Newton's Third Law of Motion

### Simple Meaning

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

### Easy Explanation

When one object pushes another object, the second object pushes back.

The push happens in the opposite direction.

### Daily Life Examples

1. When we walk, our feet push the ground backward, and the ground pushes us forward.
2. When a rocket pushes gases downward, the rocket moves upward.
3. When we jump, we push the ground down, and the ground pushes us up.
4. When a balloon releases air backward, the balloon moves forward.

### What Children Learn

Children learn that forces always work in pairs.

## Simple Activities

### Activity 1: First Law

Take a ball and keep it on the floor.

Ask:

```text
Will the ball move by itself?
```

Then push the ball gently.

Children can observe that the ball moves only when force is applied.

### Activity 2: Second Law

Push an empty bag and then push a heavy bag.

Children can observe that the heavy bag needs more force.

### Activity 3: Third Law

Blow air into a balloon and release it.

Children can observe that air goes backward and the balloon moves forward.
